Juneteenth Celebration Day - Hampton & Chesapeake

Hampton University & The Emancipation Oak

Stand beneath the historic Emancipation Oak at Hampton University, where in 1863 members of the Virginia Peninsula's Black community gathered to hear the first Southern reading of the Emancipation Proclamation. This magnificent live oak, with branches spanning 100 feet, has witnessed the journey from slavery to freedom, from Contraband camps to a premier HBCU. On Juneteenth 2026, this sacred ground becomes the epicenter where America's 250th anniversary meets freedom's celebration in a once-in-history convergence.

Cornland School Museum

Explore the Cornland School Museum, a restored one-room schoolhouse where teachers educated Black children from Reconstruction through Jim Crow. Original desks and textbooks reveal how determined teachers with limited resources lit the flame of learning despite oppression. This humble building stands as a testament to the belief that education was liberation and that knowledge could overcome even the harshest barriers to equality.

Fort Monroe - "Freedom's Fortress"

Visit Fort Monroe, the site where the first documented Africans arrived in English North America in 1619. This historic fortress became known as "Freedom's Fortress" during the Civil War when it served as a Union stronghold and refuge for thousands of enslaved people seeking freedom. Stand where history began and where the promise of liberty took root in American soil, connecting the arrival of the first Africans with the ongoing struggle for freedom and equality.

Norfolk Harborfest & Travel to Williamsburg

Norfolk Harborfest

Immerse yourself in Norfolk Harborfest, where Juneteenth celebrations merge with America's 250th birthday in an explosion of music, culture, and maritime heritage. Experience special programming that weaves African American culture into the tall ships celebration, with jazz, gospel, and blues floating across the harbor. Evening fireworks illuminate the fleet, creating an unforgettable fusion of freedom and celebration unlike anything in American history.

Colonial Williamsburg & The Bray School

Step into Colonial Williamsburg, where 18th-century America comes alive through costumed interpreters and restored buildings. Walk Duke of Gloucester Street and discover the Bray School, one of America's earliest schools for Black children, and the African Baptist Meeting House. This living museum reveals how liberty was debated while an entire population remained enslaved, presenting the complex stories of revolutionaries, loyalists, and the enslaved who built colonial America.

Sunday, June 21, 2026
Williamsburg - American Revolution & Colonial History

American Revolution Museum at Yorktown

Experience the complete Revolution story at the American Revolution Museum at Yorktown through immersive galleries and living history. Discover how Patriots, Loyalists, women, Native Americans, and enslaved and free Blacks experienced the war for independence differently. The museum boldly addresses how a war for liberty coexisted with slavery, revealing that the Revolution was not an ending but a beginning of the struggle to fulfill the promise that all are created equal.

The Williamsburg Bray School

Discover the Williamsburg Bray School, the oldest surviving schoolhouse for Black children in the United States, established in 1760. This remarkable building represents one of the earliest organized efforts to provide education to African American children in colonial America. Founded by the Associates of Dr. Bray, an English philanthropic organization, the school taught reading, writing, and Christian instruction to free and enslaved Black children. The Bray School stands as a powerful symbol of the determination to seek knowledge and education despite the constraints of slavery, demonstrating that even in the darkest times, the pursuit of learning was seen as a path to dignity and freedom.

First Baptist Church / African Baptist Meeting House

Visit the First Baptist Church / African Baptist Meeting House, founded in the 1770s by free and enslaved African Americans. This historic church represents one of the earliest organized Black congregations in America, where African Americans created their own spiritual community and space for worship, fellowship, and resistance. The church became a center of Black life, culture, and organizing, demonstrating the resilience and agency of African Americans even under the brutal system of slavery.

Freedom Park

Explore Freedom Park, the site of America's first free Black settlement. This sacred ground tells the story of free African Americans who established their own community, creating a space of autonomy and self-determination in colonial Virginia. The park preserves this important history and honors the legacy of those who built lives of freedom and dignity against tremendous odds.

  • Visit American Revolution Museum at Yorktown

Jamestown & Travel to Washington, D.C.

Visit Jamestown

Discover Jamestown Settlement, where three worlds collided in 1607 and the first documented Africans arrived in English America in 1619. Board replica ships, explore a Powhatan village, and understand how European, African, and Native American cultures shaped our nation's beginning. The exhibits honestly address the introduction of slavery and tobacco cultivation that would define colonial economy, revealing that American history requires acknowledging all who were present at its birth.

Stand on the very ground where the first documented Africans‚Äî"20 and odd Negroes" as recorded by John Rolfe‚Äîarrived in 1619, marking the beginning of African presence in English North America. This momentous arrival, occurring just 12 years after the founding of Jamestown, would fundamentally shape the course of American history. The museum's powerful exhibits explore the complex relationships between European settlers, African arrivals, and Native peoples, presenting an honest narrative that acknowledges the painful legacy of slavery while honoring the resilience, contributions, and humanity of those who were forcibly brought to these shores. Through immersive reenactments, authentic artifacts, and thoughtful interpretation, visitors gain a deeper understanding of how these three cultures intersected, conflicted, and ultimately created the foundation of what would become the United States.

Martin Luther King, Jr. Memorial

Experience the Martin Luther King Jr. Memorial, where the "Stone of Hope" emerges from the "Mountain of Despair," capturing Dr. King's belief that faith moves mountains. Read 14 powerful quotes etched in granite, from his Letter from Birmingham Jail to his final sermon.

Lincoln Memorial

Stand at the Lincoln Memorial, America's temple to democracy and site of pivotal civil rights moments. Gaze up at the 19-foot marble statue of the president who issued the Emancipation Proclamation, with his immortal words etched in stone.

Monuments by Moonlight Tour

As dusk falls over Washington, DC, the city transforms into a luminous stage where history and grandeur come alive under the moonlight. This unforgettable evening adventure showcases the city's famed monuments and memorials in a whole new light.

Washington, D.C. - American History & Culture

National Museum of American History

Discover the rich tapestry of American history at the National Museum of American History. This museum houses a vast collection of artifacts that tell the story of America, from the original Star-Spangled Banner and Abraham Lincoln's top hat to innovations in science and technology. Interactive exhibits on American culture, politics, and social movements provide a comprehensive view of the nation's history.

Explore powerful exhibits that tell the complete American story, including the Greensboro lunch counter where four Black college students staged a sit-in that sparked a movement, the Woolworth's counter that became a symbol of nonviolent resistance during the Civil Rights Movement. See artifacts from the struggle for voting rights, including items from the Selma to Montgomery marches, and discover how African Americans have shaped every aspect of American life‚Äîfrom music and culture to science, politics, and social justice. The museum's "American Stories" exhibition traces the diverse experiences of Americans across centuries, while "The American Presidency" explores how the highest office has both reflected and shaped the nation's ongoing journey toward equality.

Museum of the Bible

Discover the remarkable Museum of the Bible, where ancient manuscripts and cutting-edge technology bring sacred texts to life. Marvel at Dead Sea Scroll fragments and first-edition King James Bibles while exploring scripture's influence on civil rights leaders and American democracy.

Experience how the Bible has served as both inspiration and foundation for the African American struggle for freedom and equality. Explore exhibits highlighting how enslaved Africans found hope and strength in biblical narratives of liberation, and how civil rights leaders like Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., Fannie Lou Hamer, and countless others drew upon scriptural teachings to fuel the movement for justice. See rare Bibles owned by prominent African American leaders and discover how faith communities have been central to organizing, resistance, and the pursuit of civil rights throughout American history. The museum's immersive exhibits connect ancient texts to contemporary struggles, revealing how scripture has been interpreted, reinterpreted, and lived out in the ongoing quest for freedom and dignity.

Washington, D.C. - Memorials & African American History

Arlington National Cemetery - Guided Tram Tour

Pay respects at Arlington National Cemetery on a guided tram tour, where 400,000 American heroes rest in eternal honor. This comprehensive tour takes you through the hallowed grounds, allowing you to witness the Changing of the Guard at the Tomb of the Unknown Soldier and visit President Kennedy's eternal flame. The endless rows of white headstones tell stories of sacrifice from every American conflict, creating a profound reminder of freedom's price and the valor of those who paid it. Your guided tram tour provides expert commentary on the cemetery's history, notable gravesites, and the stories of service and sacrifice that define this sacred place, including the contributions of African American service members throughout American history.

National Museum of African American History and Culture

Embark on an enlightening journey through the National Museum of African American History and Culture, where 400 years of African American experience unfold with dignity and depth. Encounter powerful artifacts from the pain of slavery to triumphs in arts, sports, and politics, including exhibits on the Civil Rights Movement and Obama Presidency. This transformative experience connects visitors with stories of resilience and celebration that reveal how African Americans have fundamentally shaped our nation.
